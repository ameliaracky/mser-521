The U.S. Healthcare Paradox

Highest costs, Lowest returns

T he United States spends more on healthcare than any other country in the world, yet it consistently ranks near the bottom among wealthy nations for health outcomes.

This paradox highlights deep structural problems in how healthcare is delivered, accessed, and financed in the U.S.

Healthcare accounts for about 17–18% of the entire U.S. economy, while most developed nations spend closer to 9–12%.

Despite its spending, approximately one million people die annually from diet-related chronic diseases in the United States.

Poor diet is the leading cause of U.S. mortality which is directly related to malnutrition and chronic diseases including type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, obesity, and some cancers.

Almost half of U.S. adults have pre-diabetes (38%) or diabetes (11.3%), with most cases as T2D

CVD is the leading cause of death for men and women in the U.S., accounting for 695,000 total deaths in 2021

Why Does the U.S. Perform So Poorly?

Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)

are the nonmedical factors that influence health outcomes.

Lack of Universal Access

Unlike most peer nations, the U.S. does not guarantee healthcare to all residents.

High Administrative Costs

Private insurance requires massive bureaucracies for billing, approvals, and claims.

High Drug and Procedure Prices

The U.S. pays 2–4x more for prescriptions and procedures than other nations.

Weak Preventive Care System

The system prioritizes treatment instead of prevention.

A Major Piece to the Puzzle

The U.S. Food System

A central driver of America’s poor health outcomes is what Americans are encouraged, economically and structurally, to eat.

The U.S. government spends billions of dollars each year subsidizing crops like corn, soy, and wheat.

These crops become the foundation of: High-fructose corn syrup, Processed snack foods, Sugary drinks, Cheap refined carbohydrates, and Industrial meat production.

Meanwhile, fruits, vegetables, and whole foods receive very little federal support, making healthy food: More expensive, Less accessible, and Less profitable to produce.

"People are fed by the Food Industry, which pays no attention to health, and are treated by the health industry which pays no attention to food," Wendell Berry

Ultimately

The federal government is subsidizing the very foods that drive chronic disease, then spending enormous amounts of money trying to treat those diseases through the healthcare system.

This paradox needs to be more widely recognized and action must be taken in order to save the future of America.
